# Audience Inference

Infer the audience from repository evidence, not from guesswork.

## Use the strongest signals first

1. Product copy: homepage copy, nav labels, CTA text, empty states, onboarding language.
2. Information architecture: route names, dashboard sections, content models, sitemap shape.
3. User roles: auth flows, admin areas, account types, permissions, pricing tiers.
4. Core tasks: booking, browsing, publishing, reporting, purchasing, donating, learning.
5. Trust level: forms, payments, legal copy, healthcare or finance language, support expectations.

## Turn signals into hypotheses

- Primary audience: Who is the product most obviously built for today?
- Secondary audience: Who else must be accommodated but is probably not the main buyer or user?
- User mindset: Are they exploring, comparing, managing, purchasing, learning, or publishing?
- Visual priority: Should the design emphasize trust, excitement, expertise, efficiency, calm, or community?

## Map audience clues to design implications

- High-trust product: calmer color contrast, stronger hierarchy, fewer novelty gestures, more clarity.
- Lifestyle or aspirational product: stronger imagery, mood, texture, editorial typography, bolder pacing.
- Operational or internal product: denser layouts, sharper utility cues, fast scan patterns, restrained decoration.
- Youthful or community-led product: more motion, contrast, social proof, and looser visual rhythm.

## State uncertainty correctly

- If the repo has weak signals, say "likely" or "appears to target" instead of writing with certainty.
- If two audience readings are plausible, keep both and let the directions split along that line.
- Do not invent detailed personas that the codebase does not support.
